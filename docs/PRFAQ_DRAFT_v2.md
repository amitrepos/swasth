**Q3: I already own a glucometer and BP monitor. Do I need to buy the Swasth Kit?**

No. Swasth works with any standard Bluetooth-enabled glucose meter (UUID 1808) or blood pressure monitor (UUID 1810). It also supports Omron proprietary BP monitors (HEM-7140T1 and similar). If your existing device has Bluetooth, it will likely work. If it doesn't have Bluetooth, you can still enter readings manually or scan them using the camera — Swasth uses AI-powered OCR to read the number off your device screen.

**Q4: My parents are not comfortable with technology. Can they use this?**

Swasth was designed so the person least comfortable with technology does the least. The setup is done by the adult child — over a video call if needed, in under ten minutes. Once set up, the parent's daily interaction is: check sugar or BP using the device (same as they would without Swasth), and the reading syncs automatically. For users above 55, the app switches to simplified mode — a single heart indicator (green/yellow/red), larger icons, and minimal text. Total daily effort for the parent: under three minutes. The adult child handles configuration, monitoring, and alerts from their phone.

**Q5: What languages does Swasth support?**

Hindi and English at launch. All screens, notifications, AI recommendations, and error messages are fully localized. Users can switch language from settings at any time. Additional Indian languages (Tamil, Telugu, Bengali, Marathi) and Southeast Asian languages are planned for market expansion.

**Q6: How does the food photo feature work with Indian food?**

Take a photo of your plate before eating. Swasth identifies the dish — dal chawal, paratha, dosa, biryani, idli, poha, and hundreds of other South Asian dishes — and estimates carbohydrate, protein, and calorie content. After your post-meal glucose reading, the AI correlates the meal with your glucose response and tells you specifically how that meal affected your body. Over time, it builds a personalized food-glucose map for your metabolism. If the photo recognition is uncertain, you can type what you are eating in natural language. The food database expands to Southeast Asian and African cuisines before each market entry.

**Q7: What happens if my parent misses their readings for a few days?**

Swasth sends the patient a reminder at their configured morning and evening times. If the patient still hasn't logged a reading after 24 hours, all linked family members receive a notification — "Your mother has not taken a reading today." If the gap extends to 3 days, the alert escalates. The family member can then call and follow up. The ICMR-INDIAB study found that only 16.7% of Indian diabetics self-monitor regularly [9] — this feature directly targets that compliance gap by making the family an accountability layer.

**Q8: Does the AI replace my doctor?**

No. Swasth's AI provides lifestyle and behavioral guidance — what to eat, when to walk, when to take medication — based on published clinical protocols. It does not diagnose, prescribe medication, or override a doctor's instructions. It extends the doctor's reach into the 180 days between appointments. Monthly doctor reports give the physician continuous visibility so they can make better clinical decisions when the patient does visit. The AI explicitly tells users to consult their doctor when readings are critical or when patterns suggest a medication adjustment may be needed.

**Q10: What happens to my data if I stop using Swasth or the company shuts down?**

Users can export their complete health data at any time. If Swasth ceases operations, all users will receive 90 days advance notice and the ability to download their full health timeline, readings, reports, and Health Vault documents. We are also working toward ABDM (Ayushman Bharat Digital Mission) integration so that health records can flow into India's national digital health ecosystem.

**Q11: Can I use Swasth without internet? My parents live in a rural area.**

Yes. Swasth has a full offline mode. Readings taken offline are stored locally on the device using encrypted local storage. When internet connectivity resumes, the app automatically syncs all pending readings to the cloud. The health score, trends, and AI insights update once the sync completes. BLE device sync works entirely over Bluetooth — no internet required for taking and storing readings. Internet is only needed for AI recommendations, family dashboard sync, and doctor reports.

**Q12: How is this different from just buying a glucometer from Amazon?**

A standalone glucometer shows you a number. That's it. You don't know if 148 is good or bad for you, what caused it to be 148, or what to do tomorrow to make it lower. It doesn't remember your readings, doesn't see your BP, doesn't know what you ate, doesn't tell your son in Bangalore that your numbers are creeping up, and doesn't alert your doctor. Swasth turns isolated numbers into an intelligent, connected health system — device + AI + family + doctor — that drives measurable improvement over months and years.

**Q18: Why target the middle class in India and not premium customers first?**

Three reasons:

1. **Premium customers are already served.** They have Apple Watch, personal physicians, nutritionists, and executive health checkups. You'd be competing with established players for a small market.

2. **The deaths are in the middle class.** 80% of diabetes/hypertension mortality happens in low and middle income populations. This is where the volume is — 101 million diabetics in India, concentrated in Tier 2/3 cities and semi-urban areas.

3. **Pricing arbitrage through family structure.** The buyer is often an NRI or metro professional earning developed-world or metro salaries, buying for a parent in a Tier 2/3 city. You charge the child's willingness to pay, not the parent's. A son in Bangalore earning Rs 20L/year will pay Rs 500/month for his father's health monitoring without thinking twice.

**Q19: Is this just for NRIs?**

No. The NRI is the easiest story to tell an investor, but the actual market is anyone who doesn't live with their aging parents. India has 140 million internal migrants — a laborer from Bihar working in Delhi, a teacher from Lucknow posted in Pune, an IT professional from Patna working in Bangalore. They all share the same anxiety: "I'm not there, I don't know if my parents are okay, they won't tell me the truth." Add 18 million NRIs and you have 158 million potential buyers, all with parents back home who are aging. NRIs are the wedge because they have higher willingness to pay, but domestic migrants are the larger market.

**Q23: How do you prevent churn after the novelty wears off?**

Four retention loops, each reinforcing the others:

1. **Family accountability.** When a patient stops logging, their son gets a notification. The son calls. The patient resumes. This is the strongest retention mechanism — external accountability from someone the patient cares about.
2. **Health score and streaks.** Gamification that taps into daily habit formation. Breaking a 30-day streak feels like a loss. The health score gives patients a single number to improve.
3. **Doctor integration.** When the doctor starts reviewing Swasth reports and making decisions based on them, the patient can't stop logging without losing that benefit.
4. **Visible improvement.** When a patient sees their fasting sugar drop from 195 to 148 over six weeks, they attribute it to the system. Stopping feels risky — "what if my numbers go back up?"

**Q26: How do you handle liability if the AI gives bad advice?**

Three layers of protection:

1. **Clinical grounding.** Every recommendation is based on published clinical protocols (Lancet, WHO, ICMR guidelines), not generated freely by the AI. The AI selects from physician-reviewed recommendation templates based on the patient's data.
2. **Explicit disclaimers.** The app clearly states it provides wellness guidance, not medical advice. For any critical reading, the first recommendation is always "contact your doctor immediately."
3. **Fallback chain.** If the primary AI model (Gemini) returns an uncertain or potentially unsafe response, it falls back to a secondary model, and then to conservative rule-based recommendations. The system errs on the side of caution — "consult your doctor" is the default for anything ambiguous.

**Q27: Why are you focusing on India and not Europe or the US where willingness to pay is higher?**

1. **Regulatory barriers.** The US requires FDA clearance for any software that provides health recommendations tied to medical devices. The EU requires CE marking and MDR compliance. Both take 18-24 months and $500K-1M+ in regulatory costs. India's wellness-guidance classification lets us launch immediately.
2. **Competition density.** The US/EU chronic disease management space has well-funded incumbents — Livongo (acquired by Teladoc for $18.5B), Glooko, mySugr (acquired by Roche), Omada Health. In India, no one is building an integrated glucose + BP + AI + family system for the middle class.
3. **Market size.** India has 101 million diabetics. The US has 37 million. India's market is 3x larger by patient count and virtually unserved.
4. **Family structure advantage.** Indian family dynamics (children feel responsible for parents' health, joint decision-making) create a natural buyer-user separation that doesn't exist as strongly in Western markets. This gives us a unique distribution channel.

### Technical FAQ

**Q33: How does the AI engine work? What models power it?**

Swasth uses a multi-model AI architecture:

| Component | Model | Purpose |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| Health chat + recommendations | Gemini 2.5 Flash (primary) | Context-aware health coaching with 30-day patient history |
| Fallback | DeepSeek | Secondary model if Gemini is unavailable |
| Emergency fallback | Rule-based engine | Conservative recommendations based on reading thresholds |
| Image analysis (lab reports, prescriptions) | Gemini Vision API | Extracts values from photographed medical documents |
| Camera OCR (device screen reading) | Gemini Vision → DeepSeek fallback | Reads glucose/BP values from device display photos |
| Food photo identification | Gemini Vision | Identifies South Asian dishes and estimates nutritional content |
| Trend summaries | Gemini 2.5 Flash | Generates per-period AI analysis of glucose and BP trends |

The AI receives patient context for every interaction: demographics, medical conditions, medications, 30-day reading history (min/max/avg), BMI, and a rolling summary of all past conversations. It does not operate in a vacuum — every recommendation is grounded in that specific patient's data.

**Q34: How accurate is the AI compared to a real doctor?**

The AI does not attempt to replace a doctor's clinical judgment. It operates in a different domain — lifestyle and behavioral guidance between appointments. A doctor spends 2 minutes with the patient every 3 months. The AI spends every day analyzing their readings and providing specific, personalized recommendations. The doctor makes medication decisions. The AI makes "eat this, walk this, take your tablet at this time" decisions. They complement each other. The monthly doctor report is how the two connect — the AI surfaces patterns, the doctor acts on them.

**Q35: How do you handle conflicting readings or device errors?**

Swasth flags readings that fall outside physiologically plausible ranges (e.g., glucose below 20 or above 600, systolic BP below 60 or above 300) and asks the patient to re-check. For readings that are plausible but unusual (a sudden spike 100+ points above the patient's 30-day average), the AI notes it as an anomaly in the trend analysis and asks the patient to verify and re-test. The system does not automatically discard any reading — it flags, contextualizes, and lets the doctor decide.

**Q36: What BLE devices are supported? What if a patient's device isn't compatible?**

| Device type | Standard supported | Specific models |
|-------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Glucose meters | Bluetooth SIG UUID 1808 | Any standard BLE glucometer |
| BP monitors | Bluetooth SIG UUID 1810 | Any standard BLE BP monitor |
| Omron BP monitors | Proprietary UUIDs | HEM-7140T1 and similar (auto-detected by name pattern: BLESMART/HEM/OMRON) |

If a patient's device isn't Bluetooth-enabled, they have two fallback options: (1) manual entry — type the reading directly into the app, or (2) camera scan — photograph the device screen and Swasth's AI-powered OCR extracts the value automatically.

**Q37: How does offline mode work in areas with poor connectivity?**

All readings taken offline are stored in encrypted local storage (Hive database) on the patient's phone. BLE device sync works entirely over Bluetooth — no internet needed. When connectivity resumes, the app automatically flushes the sync queue and uploads all pending readings to the cloud. The patient sees their local data immediately; AI recommendations and family dashboard sync happen once online. The app gracefully degrades — it never blocks the patient from taking and viewing their own readings due to connectivity issues.

**Q39: How do you ensure the AI doesn't hallucinate or give dangerous advice?**

Four safeguards:

1. **Clinical protocol grounding.** The AI is prompted with published guidelines (WHO, ICMR, Lancet) and generates recommendations within those boundaries. It doesn't freely invent medical advice.
2. **Physician-reviewed templates.** High-stakes recommendations (critical readings, medication timing) use pre-approved response templates, not open-ended generation.
3. **Fallback chain.** Gemini → DeepSeek → rule-based engine. If the primary model returns an uncertain response, the system falls back to increasingly conservative options.
4. **Critical reading override.** For any reading classified as CRITICAL, the first and primary recommendation is always "contact your doctor or go to the nearest hospital immediately." The AI does not attempt to manage emergencies — it escalates them.
5. **Rate limiting.** AI chat is rate-limited (configurable, default 5 messages/day) to prevent over-reliance and ensure patients continue seeing their doctor regularly.
